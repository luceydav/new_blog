Analysis of Connecticut Tax Load by Income Bracket and Type

2019/01/09

Introduction

In total, this brief study finds that Connecticut residents pay close to $78-79 billion in tax (counting all forms: Federal, State, real estate, sales, FICA, Medicare, ) on adjusted gross income of $165-167 billion of income which approaches 47-48% of income. Some of the taxes such as FICA and Medicare might be considered a form of savings or insurance, and local real estate tax which fund education might have some direct offsets (if there is an excellent school district). However, these levels of tax burden approach those seen in Europe without some of the benefits such as free public healthcare or university education.

Methodology

The IRS publishes annual (SOI) tax return data by zip code grouped by income size each year [SOI Tax Stats - Individual Income Tax Statistics - ZIP Code Data (SOI)] (https://www.irs.gov/statistics/soi-tax-stats-individual-income-tax-statistics-zip-code-data-soi). We have loaded all taxes paid by Connecticut residents since 2009. It is important to emphasize that it is not possible to see individual tax returns, only all of the tax returns collectively by zip code divided by income level. With that data, we were able to add up all of the zip codes in each of Connecticut’s 169 towns in order to see most of the taxes paid in each town.

Every line item of the 1040 tax return is reflected as a variable in the dataset. For example, adjusted gross income, state and local income tax paid and mortgage interest deducted are available. It is also possible to see how many returns filed by town, income level and the range of deductions taken by group. Please see 1 below showing that almost 100% of returns above $200k itemized their returns, but less than 10% of returns below $25k. In addition, the size of the dots reflects the number of returns. Over 500k returns are in the <$25k income bracket and a little over 100k abvove $200k.

Estimate of Sales Tax, Medicare and FICA and Partial Allocation of Local Real Estate Tax to Income Groups

Several taxes are only partially available or not available at all in the tax returns, but can be estimated from the available data with reasonable confidence. For example, only a few percent of returns deduct sales tax, but this caan been estimated based on 11% of AGI for the lowest bracket down to 5% for the highest. In addition, FICA can be estimated based on 6.2% for the employee and the an additional 6.2% by the employer up to $128k. Medicare is easily calculated as 2.9% of AGI without limit.

The allocation of real estate tax paid is much more complicated because only about half of this line item is deducted on IRS tax returns by higher income taxpayers who itemize. From the work we did last week in Reviewing Fairfield County Municipal Fiscal Indicators Since 2007, we know the total amount of real estate tax for each town. Using this, it is possible to impute the real estate tax paid but not deducted by town, and then allocate that to the income groups on the federal returns for those towns.

Relatively few taxpayers with income below $25k are likely to own significant amounts of real estate, and if so, the values should be relatively low (implying low real estate taxes). In addition, the highest brackets are most likely to itemize, hence real estate taxes paid would be deducted on the returns. However, these are likely to be much higher valued homes, so only a small number not itemized could be meaningful dollar amounts. We used the following ratios to allocate to income groups: <$25k (5% allocated), $25-50k (10%), $50-75k (25%), $75-100k (25%), "$100-200k (30%) and $200k+ (5%). There is no science behind these allocations other than common sense. We do know that the approximately $10 billion paid to 169 towns should be accurately reflected, but the allocations to income groups may require further study.

Tax burden on the two lowest brackets are meaningful, but mostly composed of social security, imputed real estate and sales tax. However, as shown in 2, the total effective tax burden on the $50-75k and $75-100k groups are extremely high (close to 60%) and have been growing rapidly in percentage terms. As we will see below, these groups pay very little federal or state income tax, so local real estate taxes will make up the largest share of taxes paid in these income categories.

Figure 2: Total Taxes Paid By Type (All Towns)

It is important to note that the $50-75k and $75-100k groups have shown little or no income growth over the whole period studied, effectively not participating in the recovery. While income in the two highest brackets probably collapsed prior to the measured period (during the Great Recession), they have rebonded subsequently (by about 50%). With our data starting after the collapse, we don’t have pre-crisis income levels to compare to. This rebound has been matched with a gentle increase in taxes paid and tax burden.

While the effective tax rates of the middle income groups look extremely high, about 85% of all taxes paid by CT residents are paid by the top two brackets (28% of returns) as shown in 3. The two lowest brackets have a pretty high level of tax burden despite little federal or state income tax and some tax credits with sales, real estate and FICA contributing most of this. Counting federal, state and local taxes, residents of Connecticut are bearing quite a hefty tax load across income levels.

Conclusions

Based on this analysis, there is fodder for both political perspectives (which is the objective of this blog). Middle income groups appear to bear a significantly higher tax burden than the two upper groups as a percentage of total income. However, the two upper groups bear the predominant share of the tax load, despite representing less than 30% of taxpayers. The argument that they don’t pay their “fair share” seems to lose some strength in that context. Most of the research about taxes might be looking at the federal, state or local levels separately, so hopefully this perspective might be enlightening. It will be interesting to see the data in 2018 when the cap on state and local taxes fully enters the dicussion. It is hard to imagine a business which would grow costs in this manner, and assume that they could be successfully passed on to customers. It may be necessary to start looking for new ideas about how to reduce costs across Connecticut’s governmental infrastructure, while maintaining services. Now that the data is loaded and cleaned, a future post will look at tax burden by towns and counties to see what new insights that may reveal.
