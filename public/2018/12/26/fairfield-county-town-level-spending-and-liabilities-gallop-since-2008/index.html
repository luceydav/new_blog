Reviewing Fairfield County Municipal Fiscal Indicators Since 2007

2018/12/26

In recent weeks, we have been exploring the State of Connecticut’s municipal oversight infrastructure. Every year, each of the 169 towns is required to file an audited Comprehensive Annual Report (CAFR) the state aggregates these into the Municipal Fiscal Indicators report Municipal Fiscal Indicators - Fiscal Years Ended 2012-2016. The report is a rich source of information for drawing comparisons about municipal health. The State appears to be thinking along the same lines having recently built a benchmarking tool Municipal Benchmarking Tool.
Redwall Analytics has taken it one step further by aggregating the available Municipal Financial Indicators since 2007 which gives a picture through the crisis and then recovery over 58 fields common over the measured period. Please see the curated list of 17 variables from all Connecticut Towns in Figure 1. This table can be filtered by town and sorted by data for anyone who wishes to explore their own town’s financials. It is also possible to scroll right to see more variables not immediately visible in the table.

Figure 1: Ten Years of Key Connecticut Municipal Indicator Data

While Connecticut gets good marks for its open data initiatives, merging and cleaning .csv files in order to do most analysis is still not easy. For example, data is disclosed at unequal intervals, variable names change and new variables are added without updating past disclosures. Interesting and overlapping datasets are also included in several different locations. If there are data dictionaries, they were not easy to find, and it can be difficult to understand the context of some fields. A future post will discuss specific ways that State’s data practices could be improved from the perspective of an outside user. As mentioned in the last post, our R code for accesing the data is available by following the Github link on our home page.

Analysis of Key Town Attributes in Fairfield County

While Fairfield County has considerably more income than the State average, the subset of 15 towns here vary in size and income. In Figure 2, it is possible to hover over the line to see the town name and values per adult resident at different points in time since 2007. All towns showed a sharp dip in education spending per pupil during 2008, but have rebounded with the median now close to $20k (the State mean and median is closer to $16k). Several smaller towns towards the Northeastern side of the County (further from NYC) are seeing declining in student age population before the Great Recession (some from levels as high as 25%). Several towns in Fairfield County spend close to $25k (though some towns in other parts of the State are closer to $30k).

Although population has been declining since 2013, Connecticut had about 2% more people in 2016 than 2007. In contrast, density in the larger Fairfield County towns has been increasing much more rapidly as people have seemed inclined to leave bigger, more isolated plots further from town centers. Our hometown of Stamford in particular has been booming with apartment construction and population growth in recent years, but Norwalk has also shown growth. Most of the smaller towns have had stable density, but further Northeast, school age population has been declining rapidly from very high levels in the past. Most towns have reduced workforce sharply since 2010 and have been tracking the State averages. Stratford clearly had a bloated workforce over the period, though not the worst in the state.

Figure 2: Key Attributes of Fairfield County Towns

Tax Revenues Per Adult Resident Have Climbed Steadily, but Other Revenues Mostly Still Lagging

After a small dip in 2008, tax revenues per adult resident have risen steadily even as Grand Lists declined and never completely recovered (as shown in Figure 3). Smaller, less dense towns to the Northeast have almost double the tax levels of towns further Southwest (closer to NYC). It is notable that Non Tax Revenue in most towns has declined sharply since 2008 (though Westport is doing something right in this respect). Recent budget cuts and the need to support the City of Hartford and Bridgeport Schools have meant that fewer income tax dollars sent to Hartford are making it back to most towns in the County. Stamford, Norwalk and Stratford have shown a small amount of growth in Inter-Governmental transfers. It should be noted that there is a significant implicit transfer to every town not reflected in these numbers, due the State assuming full responsibility for Teacher pensions without charges.

Figure 3: Fairfield County Revenues Over Time

Capital Spending per Adult Resident Never Slowed During the GFC, and Education Spending Recovered Quickly

Categories of spending per adult resident can be seen in Figure 4. There has been capital spending boom with statewide annual spending rising to $15 billion from $10 billion before the Great Recession. This can be seen clearly in almost every Fairfield County towns except for Stratford. Some towns have controlled education spending much more effectively than others, especially in the smaller less dense towns to the Northeast.

It is notable that debt service in Stratford and Greenwich have increased substantially. While Greenwich has controlled Educational Spending well, Capital and Non Educational spending have boomed, and correspondingly, interest expenses have risen considerably (though manageably given the high income). New Canaan also has spent heavily and now has the highest debt service among County towns. There has been debate in Stamford about tax levels as it has invested in infrastructure to fund population growth, but it appears to shown relatively good discipline across spending categories.

Figure 4: Fairfield County Spending Over Time

Addition of Net Unfunded Pension Liabilities to Fairfield County Towns in 2015 a Shocker

Much has been written about $35 billion unfunded pension and $21b of retiree healthcare (OPEB) liabilities at the State level as legislators failed to require large enough employee contributions or to set aside sufficient reserves to cover promises. As mentioned earlier, the responsibility for teacher pensions resides with the State, but towns are still responsible for all other municipal employee pensions and OPEB (Health) benefits.

Towns started recording pension liabilities on the CAFR in 2015 with dramatic affect (as shown in the bottom left chart in Figure 5). Excellent research was done by the Yankee Institute along with the Reason Foundation taking pension liabilities into account in this report Warning Signs: Assessing Municipal Fiscal Health. In aggregate, towns in Fairfield County alone suddenly recognized new liabilities of $750 million on top of bonded debt of $1.75 billion.

Fortunately, bonded debt has been steadily falling in most towns for the last ten years. From an accounting perspective, most towns operate close to breakeven on an operating level and seem to have stable net positions with the exception of Stratford which has had notable struggles with pension obligations for some time.

Figure 5: Fairfield County Debt and Unfunded Liabilities Rising Sharply

It is important to note that these liabilities didn’t just appear in 2015 and are not attributed to that year alone. In our earlier Quick Review of State of Connecticut Spending in the R Stats Language on LinkedIn, we noted that 75% of state employee pensions are below $15k, and excluding teachers, only about 48,000 retirees are currently receiving benefits, so we are still trying to better understand how the accounting for this works. Our assumption is that an additional 50,000 teachers with higher pensions are not shown with the State’s disclosure. The sharp drop in interest rates over the last decade is likely to have a big impact increasing the present value of those obligations, especially since the legacy pay-as-you-go system meant that little was invested heading into the great bull market. If any readers can improve our understanding the numbers, it would be welcome input.
